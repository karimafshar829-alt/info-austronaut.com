The Moon

Earth's Constant Companion and the Gateway to the Stars

Basic Facts

The Moon is Earth's only natural satellite and the fifth largest moon in the Solar System. It plays a critical role in stabilizing Earth's climate and causing ocean tides.

  • Diameter: 3,474 km (About one-quarter the size of Earth)
  • Distance from Earth: 384,400 km (Average)
  • Gravity: 0.165g (About one-sixth of Earth's gravity)
  • Orbital Period: 27.3 Earth days
  • Rotation Period: 27.3 Earth days
  • Atmosphere: Exosphere (Extremely thin)

Surface & Features

The Moon's surface is heavily scarred by billions of years of impacts. It is divided into two major types of terrain: the light, heavily cratered highlands and the dark, relatively smooth plains.

  • **Maria (Seas):** Dark, basaltic plains formed by ancient volcanic eruptions. These areas appear darker because they reflect less sunlight.
  • **Terrae (Highlands):** Lighter-colored, rugged, and heavily cratered regions, representing the original lunar crust.
  • **Craters:** Impact craters cover the entire surface, ranging in size from microscopic to hundreds of kilometers across.
  • **Regolith:** A layer of loose, dusty material covering the solid rock, formed by continuous meteorite impacts.
  • **Water Ice:** Significant amounts of water ice have been confirmed to exist in permanently shadowed craters near the poles.

Origin (Giant Impact Hypothesis)

The prevailing scientific theory for the Moon's formation is the **Giant Impact Hypothesis**. This suggests the Moon formed early in the Solar System's history from the debris ejected into orbit following a massive collision between the proto-Earth and a Mars-sized body, often called **Theia**.

  • **Initial Impact:** The catastrophic event occurred approximately **4.5 billion years ago**.
  • **Debris Disk:** The energy of the impact melted and vaporized a large portion of both bodies, forming a ring of molten material around Earth.
  • **Accretion:** This material rapidly coalesced under gravity to form the Moon. This explains why the Moon's composition is similar to Earth's mantle but lacks volatile elements.

Exploration & Future

The Moon is the only celestial body other than Earth that humans have visited, and it remains a primary target for future space exploration and establishing permanent bases.

  • **Apollo Program (NASA):** Landed 12 astronauts on the Moon between 1969 and 1972, collecting hundreds of kilograms of rock samples.
  • **Chang'e Program (CNSA):** A series of successful Chinese missions, including the first landing on the Moon's far side (Chang'e 4).
  • **Artemis Program (NASA):** The successor to Apollo, aiming to return humans to the Moon and establish a long-term presence on and around it by the late 2020s.
  • **Lunar Gateway:** A planned small space station orbiting the Moon, serving as a hub for deep space missions.

Did You Know?

  • The Moon is in **synchronous rotation** with Earth, meaning its rotation period matches its orbital period. This is why we only ever see one side of it (the near side).
  • Due to tidal forces, the Moon is slowly moving away from Earth at a rate of about **$3.8\,\text{cm}$ per year**.
  • A "day" on the Moon (the time between sunrises) lasts for about **29.5 Earth days**.
  • The Moon has quakes! They are much weaker than Earth's, but they are evidence of continued tectonic activity.
